Quantum Physics: A Beginner’s Guide

Quantum physics, also known as quantum mechanics, is a branch of physics that deals with the behavior of matter and energy at the atomic and subatomic level. It is one of the most fascinating and mysterious areas of science, and its principles have revolutionized our understanding of the universe, questioning our very existence. Quantum mechanics is based on a set of fundamental principles that are different from the classical physics that governs our everyday world. Three important principles lies in quantum physics.


One of the most important of these principles is the idea of wave-particle duality. According to this principle, all matter has both wave-like, and particle-like properties, as proven by Young’s Double Slit Interference Experiment. Mr. Young's Double Slit experiment demonstrated the wave-particle duality of light. By shining a light through a single slit, a rectangular pattern was produced on the screen, suggesting that light behaved like particles passing through the slit. However, when light was shone through two slits, a diffraction pattern was observed on the screen, indicating that light behaved like waves that interfered with each other. Diffraction is the bending of waves around an obstacle and is a property of waves. This experiment provided evidence for the wave-particle duality of matter, where particles exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties depending on how they are observed.

Another important principle of quantum mechanics is the idea of superposition. According to this principle, a particle can exist in multiple states at once. For example, a particle can exist in two different energy states simultaneously. This idea is demonstrated by the famous “Schrödinger's cat” thought experiment, in which a cat is placed in a box with a vial of poison and a radioactive source. Once the monitor connected to the poison vial detects radioactivity, which has a 50/50 chance, the poison is released, killing the cat. Otherwise the cat is alive. Until the box is opened, the cat is considered to be both alive and dead at the same time. The cat's fate is tied to the state of a subatomic particle, which is in a superposition of two different states.

The third important principle of quantum mechanics is entanglement. According to this principle, particles that have interacted with each other become entangled, meaning that their wave functions become linked in such a way that the state of one particle is dependent on the state of the other particle. This phenomenon has been observed experimentally and has important implications for technologies such as quantum computing and cryptography.

Quantum mechanics has many practical applications, including in the development of lasers, transistors, and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) machines. However, it is still an area of active research and there are many questions that remain unanswered. One of the most important areas of current research in quantum mechanics is the search for a theory of quantum gravity, which would unify quantum mechanics with Einstein's theory of general relativity. While there are still many unanswered questions and controversies in the field, the study of quantum mechanics continues to be an active area of research and discovery.
